01 Abstract

Morocco enacted Law 13-21 in 2021, becoming the first historically illicit hashish-producing nation to establish a legal cannabis framework. This study examines the determinants of legal cooperative adoption among Rif Mountain farmers using a household survey of 800 households across Chefchaouen, Al-Hoceima, and Ouazzane provinces.

We estimate three nested logistic regression models with heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors and propensity score matching (PSM) to identify barriers to formalization and quantify the causal income effect of joining the legal market. Overall adoption stands at 17.1%, with significant variation by province, distance to ANRAC offices, prior arrest history, and access to legal market information.

Key finding: Farmers who joined legal cooperatives experienced a mean income gain of +31.2% relative to propensity-score matched controls in the illicit market, suggesting meaningful livelihood benefits from formalization — though the majority of Rif farmers remain outside the legal framework.

05 Methods Summary

Sample

Household survey of 800 cannabis farming households across three Rif Mountain provinces: Chefchaouen (n=360), Al-Hoceima (n=280), and Ouazzane (n=160). The unit of analysis is the household; the household head is the respondent.

Statistical Models

Three nested logistic regression models predicting cooperative adoption, all with HC3 heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors. The full model (Model 3) includes land size, distance to ANRAC, education, age, household income, prior arrests, years of cannabis cultivation, irrigation access, and province fixed effects. Average marginal effects are reported throughout.

For causal inference on income effects, 1:1 nearest-neighbor propensity score matching without replacement estimates the Average Treatment Effect on the Treated (ATT). Covariate balance is assessed using standardised mean differences (SMD < 0.10 threshold).

06 Key Findings

1. Adoption is low but regionally concentrated

Overall 17.1% of surveyed households joined a legal cooperative. Adoption is highest in Chefchaouen (18.6%) and lowest in Ouazzane (10.7%), consistent with differential proximity to ANRAC offices and pre-existing cooperative infrastructure.

2. Distance is the strongest access barrier

Households in the furthest distance quartile from ANRAC offices are significantly less likely to formalize, after controlling for land size, education, income, and criminal history. This points to an infrastructure gap addressable by expanding ANRAC field offices in remote Rif communities.

3. Prior arrests reduce adoption

Each prior cannabis-related arrest reduces the probability of joining a legal cooperative, consistent with distrust of state institutions among farmers with criminal histories. This has implications for the 2024 royal pardon of approximately 4,800 cannabis-convicted farmers.

4. Legal participation generates income gains

PSM-estimated ATT indicates legal cooperative members earn a mean income premium of +31.2% relative to propensity-score matched non-members, suggesting meaningful economic returns to formalization.

Policy implication: Expanding ANRAC office coverage, simplifying the application process, and combining legalization with criminal record relief are likely to substantially increase formalization rates among Rif farmers.
